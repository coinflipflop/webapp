Throwing a coin, we expect that either an eagle or tails will fall out. Red, sometimes it happens that the coin falls and on the edge. This, for example, happened when the judge determined the right to draw the ball before the start of the football match between Colombia and Paraguay in the 2016 America's Cup. But there, obviously, the coin was stuck in the grass. 
In probabilistic problems, such cases are not considered, considering that a mathematical coin has only two sides on which it falls with probability. 

Let's correct this injustice and give the edge of the coin equal "rights" with the eagle and the edge:  what thickness should the coin be, so that it falls out both on the side and on the edge with the same probability 1/3?

John von Neumann, who was asked this question when he got into a taxi, told the taxi driver his address and immediately answered. And, exactly as in the solution. True or not,it is unknown...
